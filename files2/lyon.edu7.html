Friday, March 12, 2021

Lyon students gain entrepreneurial experience from business competition

Senior Claire Sandy presents the business plan for her team, wtr.

 

Lyon students are venturing into a battle of the businesses this spring.

Eight teams from Lyon College are competing in the 2021 Arkansas Governor’s Cup Collegiate Business Plan Competition. Participating students get the chance to explore whether their business ideas are viable or not and learn about the new venture creation process through their team’s efforts, their work with faculty and the development of written plans and oral presentations to judges.

Students and advisors for the top three winning teams in both the High Growth/Technology and Small Business divisions will share a $100,000 cash prize pool.

 

Lyon College’s teams are as follows:

  • Rapid Tarps- Jordan Olivera, Clayton Alyea, Darius Anderson, Dustin Caplett
  • Highland Recruiting- Patrick Mitchell, Drew Sanger, Kaitlyn Roycroft, Spencer Childress, Marc Silas, Enrico Grillo
  • GrocerEase- Margaret Saad, Hunter Daley, Ray Price, Klaudija Bacovic, Dominique Loyd
  • FinanceU- Omar Gasmann, Anthony Overpeck, Seth Parker
  • We Handle It- Tom Wackens
  • Encuntro Nativo- Thei Tlo, Raimundo Cabello, Blake Carr 
  • TMG- James Dalton, Ryan Hill, Nathan Wilson, Carlos Carvalho, Ramon Patino
  • wtr- Claire Sandy, Nash Winters, Cody Steed

 

Junior Omar Gasmann, of Little Elm, Texas, is the team leader of FinanceU. His team noticed that people around their age have issues balancing their finances and wanted to address the problem at the source by starting a business that teaches high school students the fundamentals of personal finance and investing.

“Our business is based on the idea of doing more for others than can be done in return,” he said. “Our strategy boils down to having a business plan that will both make sense from an investment perspective and be respectful of the reader.”

Gasmann said his team sought out help from local entrepreneurs and venture capitalists.

“Our biggest source of help and motivation was Mike Clause. He looked over everything we had and tore it apart as fast as we could give it to him,” Gasmann said, laughing. “He helped us fully flesh out our ideas and develop our business plan and financials to where they are now!”

Senior Claire Sandy, of Batesville, is the team leader of “wtr.” Her team has developed a water filtration company to expand on the market of clean water by making it more accessible to all communities in the United States. Their product offers a two-in-one filtration and carbonation system that can be conveniently attached to the faucet of any sink. A bluetooth function will show customers the percentage of use of a filter, the temperature of the water and the amount of plastic bottles saved.

“Being able to clearly see the impact made environmentally is an important feature to us,” Sandy said. “Protect yourself and your family from contaminants, save money, and save the environment one cup at a time.”

Junior Jordan Olivera, of Dallas Texas, is the team leader of Rapid Tarps, which is producing an automatic high-efficiency tarp system for athletics departments. Since the idea is technology-based, the team has had to determine the most efficient blueprint layout for the tarp motor and recruit the right engineer for the development of the product.

“Participating in this challenge has allowed our team to gain a deeper understanding of creating and managing a new company and product,” Olivera said. “This competition has provided each one of us with more materials that investors are going to look for when they are presented with a new idea or product.”

Senior Margaret Saad, of Little Rock, is the team leader for GrocerEase, an app designed to make grocery shopping easier and more affordable. She said the competition has taught her and her teammates that there are so many factors involved in creating a business.

“Businesses require a lot of effort and supervision, with all of the cash statements and news of other businesses coming out at such a rapid pace,” Saad said. 

She continued, “Our professors have helped us realize that there are so many things we have to look for and watch out for when we are coming up with ideas and trying to run a business.”

“Starting a business can be difficult. It is exciting but very time-consuming,” said junior Patrick Mitchell, of Kaufman, Texas, the team leader for Highland Recruiting.

While developing an app to make athletic recruiting easier, even during a pandemic, his team has had to change the structure of their business model a few times. They had to set aside whole days to work on the project while balancing out the planning with their other classes.

“The biggest challenges have been figuring out how to make everything run smoothly,” Mitchell said. “I’m sure we’ll continue to improve our plan. All we have to do is trust the process.”

Semifinalists will be announced on March 15. The six finalists from each division will be announced on March 22.

Connor makes connections to complete first solo mural in Georgia

How do you finish a mural project for class when you’re no longer in the same state as your college?

For Lyon College junior Daisy Connor, the solution was networking.

Originally from Van Buren, Ark., Connor relocated to Georgia to live with her dad when Lyon closed its campus during the start of the COVID-19 pandemic. She needed to complete a solo mural for her independent study with Professor of Art Dustyn Bork, but her original plan to paint a mural for the local VFW in Batesville was suddenly not an option.

She had to find a new project in a state she had never lived in before.

Her dad had only recently moved to Georgia, so Connor had to build her own connections with the community. She went around town, visiting local businesses and telling them about the art project she had in mind.

Within a week, Total Service Solutions, a janitorial equipment company in Peachtree City, Ga., reached out to her about painting a mural on a wall inside their business.

“I got really lucky,” she said. “The company got in touch with me within that same couple of days.”

The completed mural features a sasquatch pushing one of the company’s cleaning machines through the forest.

 

Connor worked on the project three hours a day, three days a week for about a month. She started by sketching the design on paper and then sketched out large sections of it on the wall.

“I took pictures of the machines they had so I could replicate it,” she said. “I freehanded most of the sketch on the wall because the room wasn’t big enough to project it.”

Connor felt “very accomplished” after completing her first solo mural.

“It felt really cool, especially since it was in a whole different state,” she said. “I talked to Dustyn a lot about blending the colors together, but I was forced to figure several things out on my own.”

“I am impressed with Daisy and her motivation to locate a site for her mural and the wonderful work she accomplished,” Bork said. 

He continued, “She turned a hardship into a positive opportunity and did an excellent job in painting the mural. I know this is just the first of many awesome solo murals that Daisy will do!”

The experience taught Connor how to reach out to a community in order to get art projects started.

“I learned how to reach out professionally and secure jobs,” Connor said. 

She is double majoring in mathematics and art and plans to continue doing murals on the side throughout the rest of her life.

“I’ve always loved painting, so I’m never going to stop.”

Connor encourages other students to push past their nerves in order to accomplish their goals.

“Don’t wait around. Put yourself out there.”

She concluded, “Don’t be scared to do it because there are so many people in every community who love art, and they’re very welcoming and encouraging.”

Lyon announces public health ambassadors

Lyon College announced the student members of its new Public Health Ambassadors program on March 4. 

The program will aid in the College’s efforts to stop the spread of COVID-19 by promoting peer education between students. The ambassadors will act as role models, demonstrating how to follow safety protocols and incentivizing proper behavior through positive reinforcement.

Campus members and organizations that consistently follow the COVID-19 protocols can be rewarded by ambassadors with gift cards, Salty Dog drinks, pizza parties and other prizes.

Ambassadors will report to the Dean of Students and COVID Coordinator. They will conduct rounds during their weekly shifts and may be assigned specific College events to monitor.

The following students were selected by the College to serve as ambassadors this semester:

  • Klaudija Bacovic, of Igalo Herceg Novi, Montenegro
  • Daisy Connor, of Van Buren, Ark.
  • Daria Giles, of Marion, Ark.
  • Chase Griffith of Bay City, Texas
  • Conner Guntharp, Walnut Ridge, Ark.
  • Kasey Meyer, of Chillicothe, Ohio
  • Logan Richerson, of Lonoke, Ark.
  • Emerson Smith, of Little Rock, Ark.

 

COVID Coordinator Shawn Tackett believes Lyon College has chosen a great group of students to be ambassadors.

“They are very knowledgeable about the protocols and are involved in many different groups and activities on campus,” Tackett said. “This allows them to feel comfortable approaching people.”

He continued, “I look forward to working with this group of students to help keep our campus as safe as we can.”

Junior Daisy Connor wanted to make sure students’ concerns are being acknowledged in regards to the College’s protocols while helping keep the community safe through the pandemic.

“The goal is to reward those who are following protocols and to report any ideas that will help the campus run more smoothly during these times,” she said.

Sophomore Conner Guntharp saw the ambassador program as an opportunity to get some experience for a career in the medical field.

“I have always been interested in helping people with their health,” Guntharp said. “I hope to keep our spread and case numbers down so that we can keep the campus open and be able to hold events and sports.”

Freshman Kasey Meyer said she was inspired to join the program after going through the quarantine and contact tracing process herself earlier this semester.

“I want to help prevent others from having to go through that as well,” she said. “I hope to encourage my peers to follow the rules because the sooner the spread is stopped, the sooner we can get back on track.”

Junior Chase Griffith said the program’s mission is personal to him. Both of his parents would be at high risk if they caught COVID, he said, so he wanted to expedite the process of campus becoming fully safe.

“Also, a lot of my friends were seniors last year, and they were heartbroken about not having in-person graduation,” Griffith said. “I want to try to avoid that this year.”

Lyon French department holds installation ceremony for national honor society

Lyon College's newly installed Pi Omega chapter of the Pi Delta Phi National French Honor Society held their first induction ceremony on Feb. 26 while alumni and representatives from the national organization attended virtually.

 

French students at Lyon College can join a national honor society for the first time.

The French department held an installation ceremony on Feb. 26 for the College’s new Pi Omega chapter of the Pi Delta Phi National French Honor Society. The Pi Omega chapter then inducted its first official members: Assistant Professor of Romance Languages Dr. James Martell, seniors John Pruden, Tyler Brightwell and Laura Mendez and junior Wilson Borkowski.

The following alumni and friends of the College were inducted as honorary members for their investment in the French program:

  • Lauren Kuykendall
  • Alexandra Patrono-Smith
  • Alexa Overton
  • Austin Johnson
  • Jacey Flatte-Shrum
  • Brian Hunt
  • Beatrice Moore

 

Martell said he was excited to add a chapter of Pi Delta Phi to the College’s French program, crediting students like Pruden for pushing to make it a reality. 

“I am thankful we were able to get this done,” Pruden said. “It’s been in motion for a very long time.”

He continued, “This signifies the growth of the French department. Being part of this organization is a great honor and will help our program thrive.”

Freshman Madison Burrell said she looks forward to the new opportunities the honor society will provide for Lyon students.

Martell said students and alumni in Pi Delta Phi have the opportunity to network in the United States, France and other francophone countries. 

“The honor society has grants for language study in France that will help our students with their French and francophone studies,” he said. “This is a big opportunity for the College.”

Alexa Overton, ’17, said she was glad to hear that a French honor society had been added at Lyon.

“I wasn’t able to join during my time,” she said. “It’s cool that it’s becoming an opportunity like the honor societies that other departments have.”

“I’m thrilled to see the program growing and thriving,” said Austin Johnson, ’17.

Tuesday, March 2, 2021

Oliveira to give Williamson Prize Lecture on March 9 

Dr. Cassia Oliveira will give the Williamson Prize Lecture on Tuesday, March 9, at 11 a.m. in the Nucor Auditorium of the Lyon Building.

Lyon College awarded the Lamar Williamson Prize for Excellence in Teaching to Oliveira for the 2019-20 academic year. The College awards this prize annually to the faculty member considered the most outstanding in four categories: professional competence, scholarly ability, exemplary humane values and contributions to the community.

Oliveira, Associate Professor of Biology, will present “Metagenomic Analysis of Microbial Diversity in Ozark Caves.”

Her research training emphasizes genetics and evolutionary biology, focusing on understanding the process of speciation by studying traits that are responsible for reproductive isolation in the early stages of species divergence.

Since joining Lyon College in 2012, Oliveira has published a book chapter and co-authored seven scientific papers that have been published in internationally recognized journals.

“For the Williamson Prize talk, I will be presenting the results of a research project that I have been involved with for the past six years in collaboration with Dr. David Thomas and several Lyon students.”

In 2017, the group published some of their results in an article, “16S rRNA Gene-Based Metagenomic Analysis of Ozark Cave Bacteria,” in the journal Diversity. 

“Five Lyon students were co-authors in the paper,” Oliveira said. “Our work was the first culture-independent study of microbial diversity in Arkansas caves.” 

Her research has been funded by the Arkansas IDeA Network of Biomedical Research Excellence (INBRE), the Arkansas Space Grant Consortium (ASGC), and Lyon College.
