How to Write a Better, More Impactful Cover Letter  

A cover letter is a personal letter that you send, along with your resume, when you are applying to a job.  Some employers/organizations will actually REQUIRE you to provide one, especially when you are applying to online job postings.

Please check out our cover letter guide HERE as well as a sample cover letter HERE.   The cover letter is also known by other names such as Letter of Introduction (EdJoin.org uses this title), Letter of Intent and Cover Page.  No matter the name, it is still the written document used to introduce yourself to the potential employer.  It is your chance to market yourself and showcase your qualifications.  The cover letter should also entice the employer to review your resume! It is a marketing tool!  

The Main Points: 

  1. Even though some employers do not ask for a cover letter, it is always a good idea to create one and include it in your application.  It will make you stand out, so don’t skip it!

  2. Create a new cover letter for EACH position you are applying for.  You will need to tailor your cover letter to the exact requirements specified in the job posting.  A generic “one size fits all” is never recommended!

  3. Do your research and find out who the letter should be addressed to and get the correct spelling of his or her name.  If this is not possible, make sure to address it to a Human Resources Representative, Hiring Manager or Hiring Committee. (See samples.) NEVER USE “To Whom It May Concern” or “Dear Sir or Madam” as this shows you were too lazy to take the time to find out to whom the letter should be addressed.

  4. Remember – the cover letter should not be about what you want.  It should be about what you can do for the employer and how your skills and education are a great match for the posted job requirements.  Tell the employer how you meet their needs and how you will be an asset to their organization.

  5. Do not completely rehash your resume.  What you say in the cover letter should highlight the aspects of your resume that are relevant to the position.  Refer to education, paid experience and volunteer experience that will be of interest to the employer. Do not waste space on too many generic points – be specific.

  6. Keep your cover letter to one page covering three main elements: Introductory Paragraph, Body of the Letter, Closing Paragraph.

Composing the Cover Letter:  

One of the easiest ways to compose your cover letter is to use a little formula known as AIDA.  It stands for:  ATTENTION – INTEREST – DESIRE – ACTION If you remember to build your cover letter utilizing these points, you will always remember what to include.  

  1. ATTENTION The Introductory Paragraph of your cover letter should grab the employer’s attention simply by telling the employer why you are writing to them.  State what the position is and where or how you found it. Then, make a strong confidence statement (“I am confident…”) that you are a good fit. Avoid using statements like “I feel” or “I believe” – they weaken your point. 

  2. INTEREST In the next paragraph (or paragraphs), which is the Body of your cover letter, your task is to rouse the interest of the employer by convincing them that you would be a great choice.  State what makes you special by covering the ways in which you match the requirements they want. Also mention any other relevant experience or volunteer work where you used skills that would translate well to employer’s requirements.  

  3. DESIRE Keep the employer interested while creating a desire for the employer to want to bring you in for an interview.  State specific accomplishments, education or background that will make them want to meet you.  

  4. ACTION In the Closing Paragraph, take action by indicating you would like to be contacted for an interview.  Use statements like “I would welcome the opportunity to meet with you in person” or “I would be eager to be a part of your team and would like to meet with you.”  Then indicate how they can reach you by using statements such as “I can be reached at” or “Please contact me at” (give phone number) or by email at (give email address). Thank them for their time and consideration.   

Reminders:

  • In your salutation line, always use a colon, not a comma.  A cover letter is a formal business communication.  Examples:  “Dear Mr. Smith:” or “Dear Hiring Manager:”
  • Try not to begin every sentence with “I” – change it up.  Start sentences with statements like “My background includes…” or “Additionally, I have experience with…”
  • Be confident about who you are and what you will bring to the organization. Keep things positive.  Never mention what you don’t have or have never done as these statements weaken your pitch.  Rather, tell them what you DO have and what you HAVE done!
  • Never use the phrase “Please feel free to contact me…” as this implies that you are giving the employer permission to call you.  News flash – they do not need your permission!  
  • SPELL CHECK – PROOFREAD – PROOFREAD AGAIN!!
