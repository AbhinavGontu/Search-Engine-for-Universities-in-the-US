Interviewing at Your Best

In the game of the job search, the resume is the vessel that gets you an interview. The interview is the vehicle that lands you the job. Both are the two most crucial elements of winning a long, tough, arduous, time-consuming, research invested battle within yourself to secure an incredible opportunity. If by chance, you can perfect those two aspects of the job hunt, the struggle can be a lot shorter and maybe even enjoyable.

It’s really up to you on whether or not, you want to prepare, practice, and put in the work to ace your job interview and ultimately get an offer you’ve worked so hard for.  Interviews come in all shapes, sizes, and forums (pun intended). Not only will you be interviewed for when trying to land a full-time job, but for other types of opportunities too. These include volunteer opportunities, fellowships, internships, externships, promotions, leadership committees, professional memberships, running for office, and maybe even getting interviewed by parents through a courtship process. Before we jump into the nitty-gritty of interviewing, let’s first establish the purposes and goals of the job interview from both sides of the lens.

Interviewer / Employer Purpose

It is the interviewer’s job to figure out whether the candidate (you) is:

  • Motivated to do the job.
  • Skilled and trained to do the job and do it well.
  • A good fit for the organization’s work environment and culture.

Candidate Purpose

It is your goal to:

  • Display your hard-skills, experience, and interpersonal communication skills as well as your personal and professional demeanor.
  • Articulate and demonstrate your fit for the job by providing examples of your experience in the Situation, Task, Action, Result (STAR) answer format.
  • Figure out whether this job and employer fits YOU and the experience you are looking for.

  

Basic Interviewing 101s

  • Stake out the location of the interview a couple of days before the interview, if possible, and arrive at the parking lot 20 minutes early.
  • Arrive at the actual office of the interview 10 minutes early.  Before arriving into their reception area, make sure you use the rest-room to either take care of business or make sure your smile, blouse, shirt, tie, and jacket are on straight.
  • Treat every interaction with the organization’s staff as if they are part of the interview. Arrive courteously and display kind confidence and exit the building with appreciation, handshakes, and smiles.
  • Turn your cell phone off (shut it down, this means off and not on vibration mode).
  • Bring extra copies of your resume, cover letter, transcripts, writing sample, work projects in your portfolio. (Ask us how to get a free portfolio for job interviews.)
  • Greet your interviewers with a firm handshake and always look them in the eye with a smile.
  • If you are processing or even stumped by a question, it is okay to take a moment of pause and collect your calm with a few seconds of silence.  If you are still confused after those 10 seconds (max), a mature reaction of framing it could be to say:  “That is a good question, can I have a moment to think this question over?”  It is also okay to ask for clarification if you did not hear or understand the question correctly.  Not only does this buy you a little more time to open up ideas, but it also displays your maturity and poise to a degree.
  • Always have questions for when the interviewer asks, “Do you have any questions for us?” Hint: have your questions already prepared and written down.
  • With enthusiasm, close the interview by summing up your skill level, your interest in the position and organization, fit for the position and let them know that you would like to join the team. 
  • After you provide your strong closing statement, ask them what the next step is and the time-table of those steps (if they haven’t already mentioned).
  • Ask the interviewer(s) for a business card so that you can write them a “thank you” email or note.

 

Dress attire 

Business attire for both men and women – see the link below:
https://www.thebalance.com/best-interview-attire-for-every-type-of-interview-2061364 

Remember: 

  • Men: comb your hair and shave your five o-clock shadow. If you have a beard, keep it neatly trimmed, wash your hands, maintain short nails, and go easy on the cologne or forgo it.
  • Women: easy on perfume, draw your hair back to where the interviewer can see your forehead, eyes and face, maintain clean hands and well-manicured nails if your nails are long. 

Business Casual Attire
This does NOT mean casual attire. For men, business casual means long sleeve cotton dress and polo shirts along with a blazer/sport coat. For pants, chinos (Dockers TM) or wool slacks in conservative colors such as navy blue, dark grey, dark brown, and khaki. For women, blouses, sweaters, and knit-tops are acceptable. For pants, non-denim fabric that goes well with the top. A fashionable yet conservative jacket will bring the look together.  

 

Type of Interviews

There are many styles of interviews that employers utilize to assess potential candidates. They may conduct these interviews in-person, on the phone, through Skype or Zoom, and in panel discussion forum settings.

Behavioral Interview Type

Often employers will draw from a series of interviewing techniques and incorporate those into the interview itself. Most popular is asking questions based on how your past professional experiences and behavior fit into behavioral and outcome-based actions the company is seeking. Employers are evaluating the candidate’s behaviors and actions/tasks in their previous roles, to get the information they need to make a decision based on whether you are the right fit for the job.

In the behavioral interview, it is essential you know the job description “inside-and-out” as your skill set, abilities, and competencies will be measured off of the candidate they are seeking (as stated within the job description) to fulfill that role. Therefore, know yourself, know your resume, reference your most recent work-situations and work-relationships to draw from, know your success stories, know your miss-steps that you learned from and improved on, as well as knowing your communication styles with past colleagues, supervisors, and clients.

The STAR Technique

In behavioral-based interviews, you will be asked to describe a specific situation in your previous job (or life experience) and detail your behavior and actions you took to solve the problem, then share the results of the situation. Career services professionals refer to this as the STAR technique:

  • Situation
  • Task
  • Action
  • Result.

The STAR technique is a useful and systematic method to answer behavioral interview questions. Before we demonstrate the technique. Here are some questions that lend perfectly into Starfleet:

  • Describe a situation where you had to say “no” to one of your customers or clients.
  • Tell me about a time when you disagreed with your supervisor?
  • What is the most creative thing you have done?
  • Tell me about a time when you had to mediate a situation or resolve a conflict?
  • Tell me about a time when you almost threw in the towel and quit on a project?
